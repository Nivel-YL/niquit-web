> Switched from cigarettes to vaping and still craving nicotine? Here's what actually happens to your addiction when you swap one nicotine product for another.

Yes. If you're vaping, you're still addicted to nicotine. That's not an insult, it's just chemistry. Vape products contain the same nicotine found in traditional cigarettes, and your brain doesn't care whether that nicotine arrives through smoke or vapor. It binds to the same receptors, triggers the same dopamine release, and keeps the same addiction cycle running.

You probably already suspected this. Maybe you're vaping more often than you smoked. Maybe you catch yourself hitting your pod during moments you never used to smoke, like right after waking up or in the middle of a meeting. That's not your imagination.

## Does switching from cigarettes to vaping actually reduce nicotine addiction?

Here's the part that surprises most people: switching often doesn't reduce your dependence at all. It can make it worse.

A 2020 study published through the NIH found that while combustible cigarette dependence decreased after people started vaping, their total nicotine intake and overall dependence actually increased from before vaping to after. In other words, people didn't replace their cigarette addiction with a smaller vaping habit. They stacked a new addiction on top of the old one, or transferred it whole while adding extra volume.

The same research found that dual users, people who smoke and vape, had higher nicotine dependence than people who exclusively smoked or exclusively vaped. Dual users also reported greater cravings and more intense withdrawal symptoms than either single-product group.

Part of the reason is mechanical. Because there's no smoke smell, no ash, no obvious cigarette butt piling up, it's easier to lose track of how much nicotine you're actually taking in. Healthline notes that the lack of smoke makes it easier for vape users to consume more nicotine than they would have with cigarettes, simply because there's less friction stopping them. You can vape at your desk, in your car, in bed. Cigarettes forced pauses. Vapes don't.

## Is vaping actually a good way to quit smoking?

This is the question most people ask before they start vaping, and the honest answer is: not really, at least not on its own.

A 2025 study of over 6,000 U.S. smokers found that people who vaped daily quit both cigarettes and vaping at a rate 14.7% lower than people who never vaped at all. Even occasional vapers, those who didn't vape every day, quit smoking at a rate 5.3% lower than smokers who avoided vaping entirely.

Dual users do have some success quitting smoking specifically. Research from Washington University School of Medicine found that about one in five dual users (20.8%) quit smoking within 12 months, compared to 16.8% of exclusive cigarette smokers. That sounds like progress, and in a narrow sense it is. But quitting cigarettes while continuing to vape isn't the same as quitting nicotine. Often it just means trading one delivery system for another, one that's easier to hide and easier to use more often.

## Is vaping less addictive than smoking cigarettes?

There's some truth to the idea that vaping alone is slightly less dependence-forming than smoking alone. A 2020 study published on ScienceDirect found that while self-reported dependency levels were similar between smokers and vapers, nicotine appeared to have a higher reinforcing value for smokers, smokers were willing to pay more for cigarettes than vapers were willing to pay for e-cigarettes. Broader research from the NIH in 2024 confirms this pattern: people who regularly vape show the same core signs of dependence as smokers, cravings, urges, withdrawal, frequent use throughout the day, but studies generally report somewhat lower dependence levels for e-cigarettes compared to combustible cigarettes.

That gap is shrinking fast, though. A 2025 paper in Nicotine and Tobacco Research found that modern pod-style e-cigarettes deliver nicotine more efficiently than older devices, and are more reinforcing than even nicotine gum. That's a big deal, because it means the newest, most popular vapes on the market are built to create dependency faster, especially in young adults and people who never smoked cigarettes at all.

So the "vaping is the lesser evil" argument mostly applied to older, weaker devices. If you're using a modern high-nicotine pod system, you may be dealing with something just as sticky as cigarettes ever were, maybe stickier.

## What happens to your mental health if you keep vaping?

There's a mental health cost that rarely gets mentioned. A 2019 JAMA study of nearly 30,000 current e-cigarette users found that vapers had double the odds of a depression diagnosis compared to people who never vaped, and frequent vapers had 2.4 times the odds. Nicotine dependence and mood problems tend to travel together, each one making the other harder to shake.

The flip side is encouraging. Among people who managed to quit vaping, Truth Initiative found in 2024 that 90% reported feeling less stressed, anxious, or depressed afterward, and 47% said they felt more in control of their lives. That's not a small effect. It suggests a lot of the anxiety people attribute to "needing a hit" is actually anxiety the nicotine itself is causing in the first place.

## So what actually works?

The good news is that treatment for dual users isn't more complicated than treatment for smokers. Research from Washington University found that FDA-approved cessation approaches, nicotine replacement therapy combined with counseling, work just as well for dual users as they do for people who only smoke cigarettes. You don't need a separate plan for "the vaping part" and "the smoking part." You need a plan for nicotine, period.
